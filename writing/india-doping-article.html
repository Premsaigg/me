TL;DR
  • Where India Stands: #1 rank in the world for number of violations and as a percentage of tests conducted, miles apart from peers like China, France, Russia and Italy.
  • Rise in Testing: As the testing pool grew to 7,000 samples a year, the percentage of positive samples continues to be sticky, showing the likelihood of a much deeper problem.
  • 80% from 3 sports: 1 in 3 violations in most years come from Athletics alone, pointing towards a more systemic issue within the track and field ecosystem.
  • Which substances? Anabolic steroids make up more than 50% of violations. Stimulants, Hormone Modulators and Growth Factors are the fastest growing substances over the last few years.
  • A conducive environment: Lack of regulation in the health supplement supply chain, along with the added incentive of life-changing prospects of winning, undermine any deterrence on doping.
  • How do we solve: Testing will only play catch-up with the numbers, as long as we don't ensure quality control over the supplements athletes take and reduce the incentives for athletes who see sport only as a pathway.

Overview

In the latest released 2024 report by WADA, India ranks number one in both absolute number of adverse findings and percentage of samples that have tested positive. India now holds the top spot in both metrics for a 3rd year in a row.

In the last month alone, Athletes Integrity Unit (AIU) classified India as a Category A country, putting it alongside the likes of Nigeria, Russia, Ethiopia and Kenya with the highest doping risks.

These numbers clearly point to a disturbing underbelly of Indian sport, raising alarms on the integrity of results. As India sets lofty goals for ourselves to host the Olympics, it is faced with visuals of youth athletes withdrawing from competitions on seeing dope control officers. Some of the events at the 2025 Khelo India University Games had 2-3 participants, just enough to make the podium.

The anecdotal evidence and the numbers laid out here point to a serious crisis of trust in Indian sport, especially in Athletics, Weightlifting and Wrestling. Is it just testing catching up with the reality of India's health supplement market, informal supply chains and lack of broad athlete education, or are there incentives driving athletes to adopt an all-risk strategy?

The Long Story

39 to 260 in 10 Years: More Testing Leads to More Adverse Findings

National Anti-Doping Agency (NADA) and National Dope Testing Laboratory (NDTL) have gone from organisations with almost no legal backing, at the cusp of suspension, to a more robust set-up over the last 10 years. The anti-doping numbers from 2009 to 2024 shed light on a story of institutional building.

NADA, with a clear mandate of improving testing and athlete education, has expanded testing capacity from about 3,000-odd samples in FY 2014-15 to 7,000+ samples in FY 2023-24. At the same time that testing has grown, the total doping violations have also grown from 70 in FY 2014-15 to 225 in FY 2023-24.

Through these 10 years, the percentage of Anti-Doping Rule Violations (ADRV) continued to stay sticky through all cycles of rise, drop and peak in testing numbers. As the total samples fell during the Covid period with limited competitions and national lockdowns, the ADRV numbers reduced only slightly, with the ADRV rate actually increasing to 13%.

Although the Annual Report for FY 2024-25 has not been published on the NADA website, a similar trend continued in CY2024, with AAF numbers rising to 260 out of 7,115 samples as per the WADA published report.

As per preliminary data released to press by NADA and SAI for 2025, the number of AAFs have reduced to 110 out of 7,068 samples. Hopefully, these are the early signs of athlete education and enhanced testing seeping through into athlete behaviour.

A higher positive rate could also point to better intelligence and targeted testing. However, it doesn't look likely, but cannot be fully ruled out. In order to maintain a similar level of granularity at 2x sample size, NADA would need much larger resources and productivity gains than what can be inferred from their annual report. A fall in budget per test from ₹1.47 lakh to ₹0.27 lakh is contrary to what increased targeted testing would require.

The Key Years

2018, The Realisation

The 2018-19 financial year saw the testing pool increase by close to 2x, with ADRVs increasing by more than 4.5x. India went from 6th in the list of most doping violations to 3rd spot, making this the year of inflection.

This wasn't athletes waking up on a new year and deciding to dope, but reporting and anti-doping investments catching up with the reality. NADA releasing its standalone report for the first time coincided with the organisation resolving pending cases and NDTL adding new testing capabilities, a mix of factors contributing to the steep rise in anti-doping numbers.

A news report referring to the sharp increase at that time says: "Considering that at the weightlifting nationals, the top three in one weight category were stripped of their medals for doping, the recent figure looks believable." It later goes on to say: "Yes, it may not have reached the Russian proportion but it does look disturbing."

2023 & 2024, Is This the Peak?

The 2023 and 2024 data are both the good and bad years for anti-doping in India. The sample size maturing to 7,000-odd is a clear positive sign of the investment that has gone into NADA and NDTL. Although India comes nowhere close to our nearest peer, China, it closely matches the testing level of other developed countries.

Yet the number of violations continues to grow, from 217 violations in 2023 to 260 (preliminary) in 2024. India may be at the cusp of positive deterrence taking effect with years of investment in education and testing. The possibility of the peak being near should not stop India from doubling down on anti-doping.

The Breakdown, Sports

Three Sports. Half the Violations. One Uncomfortable Pattern.

When you break down the data for the past 10 years, Athletics, Weightlifting and Powerlifting make up more than half of the violations. They are exactly the sports where marginal gains in strength and recovery get you medals.

The fact that 1 in 3 violations in a given year are from Athletics points to a systemic failure in the track and field ecosystem. On the other hand, Weightlifting and Powerlifting range from as low as 2.1% to as high as 29.2%. In the case of Powerlifting, the high and low years come in succession, a pattern that is hard to explain without targeted enforcement.

Surprisingly, Rowing and Kabaddi hold a significant share in a few years. Their presence points to doping prevalence outside of strength sports. Positively, there is a reduction of violations in both sports after two high years, likely signalling the effects of deterrence and education.

The Breakdown, Banned Substances

Anabolic Steroids Dominate, But the Mix Is Shifting

Exogenous Anabolic Agents (S1.1)

Almost 1 in every 2 violations is due to Anabolic Agents, primarily in strength-based sports (Wrestling, Weightlifting, Athletics). This is the largest single category and the one most prone to contamination. That said, consistently large numbers across years with a low no-sanction rate points to more systemic, intentional use of anabolic agents, given that contamination occurs in few batches.

Peptide Hormones (S2)

EPO, human growth hormone and insulin are prescription injectables. Someone using EPO has a vial, a syringe, a refrigerator and knowledge of dosing protocols, there is almost no inadvertent pathway. A 169% increase across three years, reaching 43 AAFs in 2024, is a clear signal in the data that a portion of India's doping is organised and deliberate, not accidental.

Hormone Modulators (S4)

SERMs like tamoxifen and clomiphene, and aromatase inhibitors, are not performance enhancers in isolation. They are generally used to manage the side effects of anabolic steroid use. Their co-occurrence with Anabolic Agents dominance points to the likelihood of deliberate and medically informed usage. However, they do appear also in some supplements, adding a contamination layer to the reading.

Stimulants (S6)

The near-tripling in 2023 is partly explained by improved NDTL detection capability for substances like methylhexaneamine and clenbuterol, both of which were upgraded in WADA's prohibited list enforcement around that period. The stabilisation at 45 in 2024 suggests this wasn't a temporary testing artefact but a real plateau at elevated levels.

The Causes

What Is Driving This?

Lack of Regulation in Nutritional & Dietary Supplements

An integral part of an athlete's regime is to take supplements that assist their body both in performance and recovery. However, supplements sit in a legal grey area, they aren't tightly regulated like drugs, and are treated similarly to general FSSAI standards for packaged food.

They are highly prone to contamination and undeclared ingredients. It is estimated that around 28% of supplements contain undeclared substances. At times, the raw materials themselves are contaminated, falling outside the purview of the supplement manufacturer.

Athlete dependence on a loosely regulated market, coupled with WADA guidelines placing complete onus on the athlete for any inadvertent violation, creates a pitfall for athletes, especially those on the margins with low awareness and limited financial bandwidth.

This is evident in the cases of Para canoe athlete Rajini Jha, who demonstrated that the supplement in question was contaminated with methyltestosterone, and Javelin thrower Shivpal Singh, whose ban was reduced after evidence showed his supplement was contaminated.

Incentive of Life-Changing Prospects

In a labour market with limited opportunities for upward mobility, the prospect of government jobs and cash rewards increases the stakes of winning beyond just sports titles. Athletes are willing to put their bodies through hell just to get points at the state level that would give them the chance of competing at the national level or applying for a sports quota admission.

As this 2023 news article quotes a NADA panel member saying: "The incentives in sports are huge, jobs, prize money, scholarships. Many athletes take to doping to avail these benefits even at the cost of harming their health. They feel rewards are waiting for them."

For a long time, the lack of testing at state trials and sports quota recruitment allowed athletes to have a "risk it all" attitude. The ramp-up in testing changes this equation, from just hoping to win and qualify, to also having to evade the sample collection officer. Hopefully, this deterrence works its way through the system, paving the way for genuine candidates to claim funding and jobs.

Lack of Awareness and Informal Supply Chains

One of the other major reasons for doping is the lack of education and reliance on local informal supply chains. Athletes in India rely on local coaches, doctors and peers for information on what to consume for an ailment or recovery. There have been cases in the past where athletes have inadvertently doped because of a medicine recommended by a doctor or local physiotherapist.

A 2025 news report shows how some coaches and ex-athletes are involved in illegitimate trade of banned substances, coaxing young athletes to take growth hormone and stimulants that were marketed as essential ingredients for success.

A recent study from 2022 shows the lack of awareness among athletes and how their views differ from cohorts who have been made aware.

"A survey of 181 elite athletes in India published in 2022 showed that only 38.1% had attended anti-doping education sessions hosted by NADA or their federation. Overall, 67.4% were aware of NADA or WADA, and 53.6% were aware of suspensions for anti-doping rule violations. There was a significant increase in awareness of the risks reported by those who attended these sessions."

Star S. The quest for harmonisation in anti-doping: an Indian perspective. Int Sports Law J.

This clearly emphasiseses the need for more education and training of athletes at a grassroots level, something NADA has been doubling down on, with educational sessions growing from 90 in FY 2018-19 to 241 in FY 2023-24.

The Process and Bottlenecks

From Sample to Sanction: A Lengthy Road

The process from an initial adverse finding to a final sanction can be lengthy. Once there is an adverse finding (AAF) in a sample, it is evaluated and confirmed as an ADRV with provisional suspension, which can then be contested by the athlete in front of the Anti-Doping Disciplinary Panel (ADDP), and further appealed in front of the Anti-Doping Appeals Panel (ADAP).

In FY 2023-24, the median time for a sanction by ADDP was 5.7 months, with a few exceptional cases exceeding up to 32 months.

Overall, in the 10 years for which we have data, there have been 928 ADDP rulings with only 17% going to appeal, meaning most athletes accept the ADDP ruling. There have been fewer than 10 cases cleared or given no-sanction in 10 years, meaning any provisional sanction after an adverse finding stays in some form or other.

Conclusion

A Systemic Problem That Requires a Systemic Response

The pattern is difficult to explain as a collection of isolated violations. The concentration in strength sports, the substance profile, and its persistence despite increased testing are all consistent with a system that enables doping, not just a few bad actors who exploit it.

The system is shaped by two forces: strong incentives to win, and weak regulation over supplements and other inputs athletes consume. In such an environment, the line between accidental exposure and deliberate use begins to blur, and enforcement ends up reacting to outcomes rather than addressing causes.

Expanding testing and awareness will help, but primarily at the level of detection, not prevention. Detection, by itself, cannot keep pace with a system that continues to produce violations.

Unless incentives are corrected and the supplement ecosystem is brought under credible quality control, India's anti-doping effort will remain reactive, tracking a problem it does not yet fully control.

"When detection keeps pace with violations, the problem is not just enforcement, it's the incentives."
