How I Work

Problems I've solved

I have spent six years working on problems that do not have obvious answers, often in financial products where progress depended on judgement rather than complete information. Here's how I typically approach these situations:

Bringing Structure to Ambiguity

Context

Requests rarely arrived as clear problem statements. They were often framed as feature ideas or urgent asks, with limited clarity on the underlying problem or how they fit within broader goals.

My role

Before discussing solutions, I focused on clarifying what we were actually trying to solve. This meant breaking requests down to their core problem, identifying assumptions, and filling in missing context through conversations with stakeholders and subject matter experts. Where necessary, I pushed back on premature solutions and reframed discussions around intent, constraints, and user impact.

Outcome

Teams were able to agree on the right problem before work began. This reduced rework and unnecessary debate, and allowed us to make early decisions about scope and priorities.

This approach tends to work well in environments where requirements evolve and trade-offs need to be made deliberately.

Coordinating Across Teams

Context

I was brought into a high-pressure project where communication had broken down. Stakeholders were misaligned, requirements were unclear, and progress had slowed due to the absence of a single point of coordination.

My role

I took responsibility for understanding what each group needed and where dependencies existed. Over time, I became the primary point of contact, translating between teams, documenting decisions, and making judgement calls to keep work moving. This was not a formally assigned role, but one I stepped into as gaps became apparent.

Outcome

The project stabilised. Ownership became clearer, decisions stopped stalling, and teams knew where to go for alignment when issues arose.

This kind of work has shaped how I think about operational roles, especially where multiple stakeholders and tight timelines intersect.

Supporting Decisions Under Pressure

Context

We were integrating with an industry leader's platform following an extended partnership period. Once technical work began, we had a short window to demonstrate a working integration, with limited documentation available.

My role

I worked closely with the lead developer to map how information flowed between the two systems, addressing gaps where documentation was incomplete. At a key decision point, I advised against a partial integration that would have met the immediate deadline, and instead recommended completing the core integration upfront to reduce long-term risk and rework.

Outcome

We delivered the first phase on time. Because the underlying integration was done early, subsequent phases progressed more smoothly, with fewer unknowns and less rework.

It reinforced how I approach decisions where short-term gains need to be weighed against longer-term costs.

Why Sports

The honest version

Cricket was first. I've followed it since childhood, the way most Indians do. Tennis came later, watching Federer and Nadal stretch each other to the limit. Cycling pulled me in during the 2020 Tour de France, stuck at home with nowhere to go. Rugby, golf, and others followed, each revealing something different about competition, strategy, and what it takes to sustain excellence.

What draws me beyond watching is the business underneath: how tournaments come together, how organisations balance performance and commercial reality, how fan experiences get designed, how grassroots systems create pathways for talent.

This isn't a detour. I'm betting on sports as the direction I want to build towards.
