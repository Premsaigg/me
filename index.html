Why Sport

Why sport

What draws me to sport isn't fandom, though I follow cycling, tennis, cricket, and rugby closely. It's the machinery underneath. Sport is one of the few industries where the product is genuine, sustained, emotionally-invested attention — and the economic structures built around that are fascinating to pull apart. Who holds leverage, where value gets captured, how incentives shape decisions on and off the field: these are questions I return to constantly, not as academic exercises but as real puzzles about where the industry is heading.

But there's a second pull, harder to frame professionally. Athletes and organisations don't just compete — they mean something to people. They shape how communities see themselves, how fans anchor identity, how stories travel between generations. The All Blacks aren't just a rugby team. What Indian cricket became for a generation wasn't just sport. This duality — the cold mechanics of the business and the deeply human stakes of the story — is what makes sport a genuinely unusual place to work.

How I Work

I question the brief before the work begins

I become the bridge between teams

I push for the harder call when it matters

I find the actual problem first

Most requests I've worked on didn't arrive as problems. They arrived as solutions — a feature to build, a report to produce, a process to overhaul. Before anything else, I'd stop and ask what we were actually trying to solve, and for whom.

This sometimes frustrated people. But more often, it surfaced a completely different problem — one faster to address, or that needed a different person in the room, or had been misdiagnosed for months. Questioning the brief before touching the work isn't overthinking. It's how you avoid building the right answer to the wrong question.

Fast-moving environments can't afford to execute in the wrong direction. The ability to pause, reframe, and align early saves far more than it costs.

I hold things together when they start to pull apart

There was a project I was brought into after communication had already broken down. Multiple teams, unclear ownership, a timeline slipping. Nobody was to blame — the situation had just outgrown the structure around it.

I took on the work of keeping information flowing: understanding what each group needed, translating between them, following up until things moved. Over time, trust built — and with it came permission to make judgement calls, to unblock decisions that had been sitting in no-man's land.

Coordination isn't process — it's relationships built before you need them.

I think past the immediate fix

During a time-sensitive integration, we had incomplete documentation and a narrow window to deliver. The path of least resistance was a workaround — something that would get us through the day and create problems six months later.

I pushed back. Clearly, not loudly. We took the slightly harder path, delivered the first phase on time, and every subsequent phase was smoother for it.

Pressure doesn't always demand speed. Sometimes it demands the clarity to separate what's urgent from what's important — and the confidence to say so.

None of this happened in sport. But ambiguity, coordination breakdowns, and high-stakes decisions with incomplete information — these aren't unique to finance. They're the texture of any complex, fast-moving environment. I'd like to bring them to one.
